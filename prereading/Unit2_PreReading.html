When Doing Everything Right Is the Wrong Thing
Understanding Why Good Management Causes Great Firms to Fail
Key Framework: The Five Laws of Disruption

The most unsettling insight in Clayton Christensen’s work is not that companies fail. Companies fail all the time, for obvious reasons: bad products, poor management, insufficient capital, market shifts. What Christensen demonstrated is that companies can fail because they are well managed — that the very practices which make firms successful in sustaining innovation make them structurally incapable of responding to disruptive innovation.

This paradox is captured in what this course calls the Five Laws of Disruption, derived from Christensen’s original framework. Each law describes a structural mechanism, not a management mistake.

The first law — Resource Dependence — states that companies depend on their customers and investors for resources. This sounds obvious, but its implication is radical: if your best customers do not want the disruptive product, and your investors will punish you for pursuing low-margin opportunities, then rational resource allocation will consistently reject disruptive projects.

The second law states that small markets do not solve large companies’ growth needs. A company generating $40 billion in annual revenue needs $4 billion in new revenue simply to maintain 10% growth. No emerging disruptive market is that large. By the time the market is big enough to matter, the window for entry has closed.

The third law argues that markets that do not exist cannot be analysed. Every tool in the conventional management toolkit — market research, financial projections, competitive analysis — assumes an existing market with measurable data. Disruptive markets have neither. Companies that demand analytical certainty before acting will never act.

The fourth law observes that an organisation’s capabilities define its disabilities. Resources, processes, and values (RPV) that make a firm excellent at sustaining innovation simultaneously make it incapable of pursuing disruption. The capability is the disability.

The fifth law states that technology supply does not equal market demand. Technologies improve faster than customer needs evolve, meaning incumbents inevitably overshoot — building products that are better than customers require. This overshoot is exactly where disruptors enter.

Together, these five laws explain why disruption is a structural problem, not a management failure. The solution is not better management of the existing business — it is structural separation, autonomous units, and discovery-driven planning.
