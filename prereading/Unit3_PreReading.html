The Chasm Is Real — Most Ideas Die There
Understanding Diffusion as the Bridge Between Invention and Impact
Key Framework: Rogers’ Diffusion Curve

Having a good idea is not the hard part. The hard part is getting anyone to adopt it. Everett Rogers, an American communication theorist, spent his career studying why some innovations spread rapidly while others stall or die. His answer, published in Diffusion of Innovations (1962, updated to a fifth edition in 2003), remains one of the most cited frameworks in innovation studies.

Rogers’ central insight is that adoption is not a single event but a process that unfolds across a population in a predictable bell-curve pattern. The population divides into five groups based on their willingness to adopt: Innovators (2.5%), Early Adopters (13.5%), Early Majority (34%), Late Majority (34%), and Laggards (16%). Each group has fundamentally different motivations, risk tolerances, and decision criteria.

The practical implication is that the marketing and product strategy that works for early adopters will fail with the early majority. Visionaries buy potential; pragmatists buy proof. Visionaries tolerate bugs; pragmatists demand reliability. This gap between early adopters and the early majority is what Geoffrey Moore famously called “the chasm” — and it is where most innovative products die.

Moore’s contribution was to show that crossing the chasm requires a fundamentally different strategy: target a single niche segment, build a complete “whole product” for that segment, and use that reference base to expand into adjacent segments. The bowling alley, not the mass market, is where the early majority is won.

For entrepreneurs, the diffusion curve is not merely descriptive. It is a strategic planning tool. It tells you who your first customers should be, what product they need, what evidence will convince the next group, and how long the journey from invention to mainstream adoption will take. Most venture failures are not product failures — they are diffusion failures.
