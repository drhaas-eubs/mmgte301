Rogers’ Diffusion Curve

A model describing how innovations spread through populations in a predictable bell-curve pattern, segmenting adopters into five categories with distinct motivations and decision criteria.
Bell-curve adoption: Innovators to Laggards
Innovators (2.5%) → technology enthusiasts willing to take risks
Early Adopters (13.5%) → visionaries who see strategic potential
Early Majority (34%) → pragmatists requiring proven solutions and references
Late Majority (34%) → conservatives adopting under peer pressure
Laggards (16%) → sceptics who resist change until unavoidable
Reference

Rogers, E.M. (2003) Diffusion of Innovations. 5th edn. New York: Free Press.

Slibrary 9 — Diffusion of Innovation

Adopter Categories

A classification of innovation adopters into five psychographic segments — each requiring fundamentally different marketing messages, evidence types, and product completeness levels.
Five segments with distinct motivations and buying criteria
Innovators → buy technology for its own sake, tolerate bugs and incomplete products
Early Adopters → buy vision and strategic advantage, accept risk for competitive gain
Early Majority → buy proven solutions with references, demand whole products
Late Majority → buy standards, need simplicity and peer validation
Laggards → buy only when forced, resist until the old way is no longer available
Reference

Rogers, E.M. (2003) Diffusion of Innovations. 5th edn. New York: Free Press, pp. 267–299.

Slibrary 9 — Diffusion of Innovation

Rate of Adoption Factors

Rogers’ five perceived attributes that determine the speed at which an innovation diffuses: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability.
Five attributes that determine how fast an innovation spreads
Relative advantage → how much better than what it replaces
Compatibility → fit with existing values, experiences, and needs
Complexity → ease of understanding and use (inversely related to adoption)
Trialability → ability to experiment on a limited basis before committing
Observability → visibility of results to others, enabling social proof
Reference

Rogers, E.M. (2003) Diffusion of Innovations. 5th edn. New York: Free Press, pp. 221–258.

Slibrary 9 — Diffusion of Innovation

Innovation-Decision Process

A five-stage model describing the mental process individuals go through when deciding whether to adopt an innovation: knowledge, persuasion, decision, implementation, and confirmation.
Five stages from awareness to confirmation of adoption
Knowledge → becoming aware the innovation exists
Persuasion → forming a favourable or unfavourable attitude
Decision → choosing to adopt or reject
Implementation → putting the innovation into use
Confirmation → seeking reinforcement or reversing the decision
Reference

Rogers, E.M. (2003) Diffusion of Innovations. 5th edn. New York: Free Press, pp. 168–218.

Slibrary 9 — Diffusion of Innovation

Chasm Theory

Moore’s model identifying the critical discontinuity between visionaries (early adopters) and pragmatists (early majority) — the point where most innovative products fail because they cannot make the transition.
The dangerous gap between early adopters and early majority
The Chasm → a psychographic gap, not a market size gap
Visionaries vs. Pragmatists → fundamentally different buying criteria
Whole product → pragmatists need complete solutions, not raw technology
Beachhead strategy → dominate one niche completely before expanding
Reference

Moore, G.A. (2014) Crossing the Chasm. 3rd edn. New York: HarperBusiness.

Slibrary 10 — Ideation Methods

Design Thinking

A human-centred innovation methodology developed at Stanford d.school and popularised by IDEO, using empathy, experimentation, and iteration to solve complex problems.
Human-centred five-stage process: Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test
Empathise → observe and engage with users to understand their needs deeply
Define → synthesise observations into a clear problem statement
Ideate → generate a wide range of possible solutions without judgment
Prototype → build quick, low-fidelity representations of ideas
Test → put prototypes in front of users, learn, and iterate
Reference

Brown, T. (2009) Change by Design: How Design Thinking Transforms Organizations and Inspires Innovation. New York: HarperBusiness.

Slibrary 10 — Ideation Methods

SCAMPER Technique

A structured ideation technique using seven action verbs as creative prompts to transform existing products, services, or processes into new innovations.
Seven prompts: Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse
Substitute → what components, materials, or people can be replaced?
Combine → what ideas, functions, or products can be merged?
Adapt → what existing solutions from other contexts can be applied?
Modify/Magnify → what can be enlarged, exaggerated, or changed in form?
Put to other use / Eliminate / Reverse → repurpose, simplify, or invert
Reference

Eberle, B. (1996) Scamper: Creative Games and Activities for Imagination Development. Waco, TX: Prufrock Press.

Slibrary 10 — Ideation Methods

Brainstorming & Brainwriting

Osborn’s brainstorming rules (defer judgment, go for quantity, build on others’ ideas) paired with brainwriting (silent written idea generation) to overcome groupthink and production blocking.
Group ideation techniques for quantity over quality in idea generation
Defer judgment → separate idea generation from idea evaluation
Go for quantity → volume produces variety, variety produces breakthroughs
Build on ideas → use others’ ideas as springboards (Yes, and…)
Brainwriting → silent, written generation eliminates groupthink and anchoring
Reference

Osborn, A.F. (1953) Applied Imagination: Principles and Procedures of Creative Problem-Solving. New York: Charles Scribner’s Sons.

Slibrary 10 — Ideation Methods

Lateral Thinking

De Bono’s approach to creative thinking that deliberately disrupts logical, sequential (vertical) thinking by introducing provocations, random entry points, and alternative perspectives.
Deliberate techniques for breaking out of established thought patterns
Provocation → deliberately making statements that are logically wrong to trigger new ideas
Random entry → using an unrelated stimulus to force new connections
Challenge → questioning why things are done a certain way, not to criticise but to explore
Movement → using a provocative idea as a stepping stone rather than judging it
Reference

De Bono, E. (1970) Lateral Thinking: A Textbook of Creativity. London: Ward Lock Educational.

Slibrary 10 — Ideation Methods

Morphological Analysis

Zwicky’s method for systematically exploring all possible solutions to a multi-dimensional problem by decomposing it into independent parameters and combining them in a matrix.
Systematic exploration of all possible combinations of solution dimensions
Decomposition → break the problem into independent dimensions or parameters
Matrix construction → list all possible values for each dimension
Combination → systematically explore combinations across dimensions
Evaluation → filter combinations for feasibility, novelty, and value
Reference

Zwicky, F. (1969) Discovery, Invention, Research Through the Morphological Approach. New York: Macmillan.

Opportunity-Feasibility Matrix

A strategic screening tool plotting potential opportunities on two axes — market attractiveness (size, growth, timing) and execution feasibility (team capability, resources, technology readiness).
Two-axis evaluation: market attractiveness vs. team capability
Market attractiveness → size, growth rate, competitive intensity, timing
Execution feasibility → team skills, technology readiness, resource availability
Go zone → high attractiveness + high feasibility = pursue immediately
Watch zone → high attractiveness + low feasibility = build capability first
Reference

Timmons, J.A. and Spinelli, S. (2009) New Venture Creation: Entrepreneurship for the 21st Century. 8th edn. Boston: McGraw-Hill.

Slibrary 11 — Opportunity Assessment

PESTLE Analysis

A macro-environmental scanning framework examining Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, and Environmental factors that create or constrain entrepreneurial opportunities.
Six macro-environmental factors shaping opportunities and threats
Political → government stability, trade policy, regulation changes
Economic → growth rates, interest rates, inflation, unemployment
Social → demographics, cultural trends, lifestyle changes
Technological → innovation rates, automation, digital infrastructure
Legal → employment law, consumer protection, intellectual property
Environmental → climate regulation, sustainability requirements, resource scarcity
Reference

Aguilar, F.J. (1967) Scanning the Business Environment. New York: Macmillan.

Slibrary 11 — Opportunity Assessment

Trend Mapping Framework

A strategic foresight tool for identifying how multiple converging trends — technological, social, economic, and regulatory — intersect to create windows of disruptive opportunity.
Identifying converging trends that create disruptive opportunity windows
Trend identification → scanning for signals of change across PESTLE dimensions
Convergence analysis → mapping where multiple trends intersect
Opportunity windows → time-limited openings created by trend convergence
Scenario planning → exploring multiple futures based on trend trajectories
Reference

Rohrbeck, R. and Gemunden, H.G. (2011) ‘Corporate Foresight: Its Three Roles in Enhancing the Innovation Capacity of a Firm’, Technological Forecasting and Social Change, 78(2), pp. 231–243.

Slibrary 11 — Opportunity Assessment

Market Research Process

A structured approach to gathering and analysing market data: defining the research problem, designing the methodology, collecting and analysing data, and translating findings into actionable business decisions.
Systematic four-stage process from problem definition to actionable insight
Problem definition → what specific question must be answered to reduce risk?
Research design → qualitative (interviews, observation) vs. quantitative (surveys, data)
Data collection → primary (original research) vs. secondary (existing data)
Analysis and action → translating findings into strategic recommendations
Reference

Malhotra, N.K. (2019) Marketing Research: An Applied Orientation. 7th edn. Harlow: Pearson.

Slibrary 11 — Opportunity Assessment

Customer Discovery

Steve Blank’s structured process for getting out of the building and testing business model hypotheses through direct customer interaction — the foundation of the Lean Startup movement.
Blank’s four-step process for testing business model hypotheses with real customers
State hypotheses → articulate assumptions about customer segments and problems
Test the problem → interview potential customers about their actual needs and pain points
Test the solution → present the proposed solution and measure response
Pivot or proceed → revise the business model based on evidence or advance to validation
Reference

Blank, S. and Dorf, B. (2012) The Startup Owner’s Manual: The Step-by-Step Guide for Building a Great Company. Pescadero, CA: K&S Ranch.

Desirability-Feasibility-Viability Triangle

IDEO’s three-circle model for evaluating innovation ideas: desirability (human need), feasibility (technical possibility), and viability (business sustainability) — successful innovations sit at the intersection of all three.
Three-lens innovation filter: do people want it, can we build it, will it make money?
Desirability → does a real human need exist? Will people actually use this?
Feasibility → can this be built with available or foreseeable technology?
Viability → can this sustain itself financially as a business?
Intersection → innovation sweet spot where all three circles overlap
Reference

Brown, T. (2009) Change by Design. New York: HarperBusiness, pp. 18–19.

Slibrary 12 — From Idea to Venture

Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

The version of a new product that allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning about customers with the least effort — not a minimal product, but a minimal experiment.
The smallest product that enables validated learning about customers
Learning vehicle → the MVP is a tool for learning, not a product launch
Hypothesis testing → each MVP tests a specific assumption about the business model
Concierge MVP → manually delivering the service to learn before building technology
Wizard of Oz → simulating automation with human effort behind the scenes
Reference

Ries, E. (2011) The Lean Startup. New York: Crown Business, pp. 93–120.

Slibrary 12 — From Idea to Venture

Lean Canvas

Ash Maurya’s adaptation of the Business Model Canvas for startups, replacing partners and activities with problem and unfair advantage — designed to be completed in 20 minutes and revised weekly.
One-page business model for startups, emphasising problem-solution fit
Problem → top 3 problems your target customers face
Solution → top 3 features addressing those problems
Unique Value Proposition → single clear message explaining why you are different
Unfair Advantage → something that cannot be easily copied or bought
Reference

Maurya, A. (2012) Running Lean: Iterate from Plan A to a Plan That Works. 2nd edn. Sebastopol, CA: O’Reilly Media.

Slibrary 12 — From Idea to Venture

Problem-Solution Fit

The stage of venture development where founders confirm that the problem they intend to solve is real, important, and underserved — a prerequisite for product-market fit.
Validating that a real problem exists before building a solution
Problem validation → evidence that the problem exists and matters to a defined segment
Solution validation → evidence that the proposed solution addresses the problem effectively
Early adopter identification → finding the segment with the most urgent version of the problem
Pivot trigger → when evidence shows the problem or solution hypothesis is wrong
Reference

Maurya, A. (2012) Running Lean. 2nd edn. Sebastopol, CA: O’Reilly Media, pp. 7–28.

Slibrary 12 — From Idea to Venture

Product-Market Fit Pyramid

Dan Olsen’s hierarchical model showing how product-market fit is achieved by aligning five layers: target customer, underserved needs, value proposition, feature set, and user experience.
Five layers from target customer to UVP, building systematic fit
Target customer → define the specific segment you are building for
Underserved needs → identify gaps in how current solutions serve this segment
Value proposition → articulate how your product addresses these needs better
Feature set → the minimum set of features that delivers the value proposition
UX design → the interface and experience that makes the features usable
Reference

Olsen, D. (2015) The Lean Product Playbook: How to Innovate with Minimum Viable Products and Rapid Customer Feedback. Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons.
