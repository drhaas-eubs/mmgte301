Slibrary 5 — The Five Laws of Disruption

Law 1: Resource Dependence

Companies depend on customers and investors for resources, making them unable to invest in disruptive technologies that those stakeholders do not yet want.
Firms are captive to the resource needs of their current customers and investors
Customer control → existing customers determine resource allocation priorities
Investor pressure → shareholders reward sustaining investments, punish uncertainty
Resource diversion → disruptive projects starved of funding and talent
Rational rejection → managers correctly reject low-margin disruptive opportunities
Reference

Christensen, C.M. (2016) The Innovator’s Dilemma: When New Technologies Cause Great Firms to Fail. Boston, Mass.: Harvard Business Review Press, pp. 30–53.

Slibrary 5 — The Five Laws of Disruption

Law 2: Small Markets Don’t Solve Growth Needs

Large companies need large new revenue streams to maintain growth rates, making small emerging markets structurally unattractive even when they represent the future.
Emerging markets are too small to satisfy large firms’ growth requirements
Growth imperative → a $40bn company needs $4bn in new revenue just to grow 10%
Market size mismatch → disruptive markets start small by definition
Opportunity cost → managers rationally pursue large, visible markets instead
Waiting trap → by the time the market is large enough, entrants own it
Reference

Christensen, C.M. (2016) The Innovator’s Dilemma. Boston, Mass.: Harvard Business Review Press, pp. 54–73.

Slibrary 5 — The Five Laws of Disruption

Law 3: Markets That Don’t Exist Can’t Be Analysed

Conventional market research and planning tools fail for disruptive innovations because the target market does not yet exist, requiring discovery-driven rather than analytical planning.
Disruptive markets cannot be researched — they must be discovered
Analysis paralysis → demanding data for a market that has no data yet
Discovery-driven planning → assumptions replace forecasts as the planning unit
Failure as learning → early market failures are information, not defeats
Emergent strategy → the right strategy emerges from action, not from analysis
Reference

Christensen, C.M. (2016) The Innovator’s Dilemma. Boston, Mass.: Harvard Business Review Press, pp. 74–95.

Slibrary 5 — The Five Laws of Disruption

Law 4: Capabilities Define Disabilities

An organisation’s capabilities — its resources, processes, and values (RPV) — simultaneously define what it cannot do, creating structural blind spots that make disruption invisible to incumbents.
What makes a firm great at sustaining innovation makes it bad at disruption
Resources → what the firm has (people, technology, cash, brands)
Processes → how the firm does things (decision-making, budgeting, planning)
Values → what the firm prioritises (margins, market size, customer type)
Disability mirror → each capability creates a corresponding inability
Reference

Christensen, C.M. and Raynor, M.E. (2003) The Innovator’s Solution: Creating and Sustaining Successful Growth. Boston, Mass.: Harvard Business School Press, pp. 177–206.

Slibrary 5 — The Five Laws of Disruption

Law 5: Technology Supply ≠ Market Demand

The pace of technological progress frequently exceeds the rate at which customers can absorb it, creating the overshoot that opens the door for simpler, cheaper disruptive alternatives.
Technology improves faster than customers can absorb — creating overshoot
Performance overshoot → products become better than customers need
Basis of competition shifts → from functionality to reliability to convenience to price
Disruptive window → overshoot creates space for simpler, cheaper alternatives
Modularisation → overshooting triggers shift from integrated to modular architectures
Reference

Christensen, C.M. (2016) The Innovator’s Dilemma. Boston, Mass.: Harvard Business Review Press, pp. 171–197.

Slibrary 6 — Entrepreneurial Mindset

Effectuation Process

Sarasvathy’s model of expert entrepreneurial thinking: instead of starting with a goal and finding means, effectuators start with their means (who they are, what they know, whom they know) and discover possible goals.
Start with means, not goals — who you are, what you know, whom you know
Bird-in-hand → start with what you have, not what you need
Affordable loss → invest only what you can afford to lose, not expected return
Crazy quilt → build partnerships with self-selecting stakeholders
Lemonade → leverage surprises rather than avoiding them
Pilot-in-the-plane → focus on controllable aspects of an unpredictable future
Reference

Sarasvathy, S.D. (2001) ‘Causation and Effectuation: Toward a Theoretical Shift from Economic Inevitability to Entrepreneurial Contingency’, Academy of Management Review, 26(2), pp. 243–263.

Slibrary 6 — Entrepreneurial Mindset

Bricolage

The entrepreneurial practice of making do by applying combinations of resources at hand to new problems and opportunities — creating something from nothing through resourceful recombination.
Making do with what you have — recombining existing resources for new purposes
Making do → refusing to be constrained by conventional resource limitations
Recombination → using existing resources for purposes they were not designed for
Community handiwork → drawing on diverse networks for materials and skills
Contrast with optimisation → bricoleurs create; optimisers refine
Reference

Baker, T. and Nelson, R.E. (2005) ‘Creating Something from Nothing: Resource Construction through Entrepreneurial Bricolage’, Administrative Science Quarterly, 50(3), pp. 329–366.

Slibrary 6 — Entrepreneurial Mindset

Cognitive Adaptability

The extent to which entrepreneurs are dynamic, flexible, self-regulating, and engaged in generating multiple decision frameworks — sensing and processing changes in their environment and acting on them.
Dynamic, flexible thinking that senses change and generates multiple decision frameworks
Metacognition → thinking about your own thinking processes
Multiple frameworks → generating alternative mental models for the same situation
Environmental scanning → continuously sensing changes in the competitive landscape
Self-regulation → adjusting strategy and behaviour based on feedback
Reference

Haynie, J.M. and Shepherd, D.A. (2011) ‘Toward a Theory of Discontinuous Career Transition’, Entrepreneurship Theory and Practice, 35(5), pp. 817–844.

Slibrary 6 — Entrepreneurial Mindset

Entrepreneurial Self-Efficacy

An individual’s belief in their ability to successfully perform entrepreneurial roles and tasks — a stronger predictor of venture creation than personality traits, demographics, or education.
Belief in one’s ability to perform entrepreneurial tasks successfully
Task-specific confidence → not general optimism but domain-specific belief
Five dimensions → marketing, innovation, management, risk-taking, finance
Mastery experiences → prior success is the strongest source of self-efficacy
Bandura’s framework → self-efficacy shapes effort, persistence, and resilience
Reference

Chen, C.C., Greene, P.G. and Crick, A. (1998) ‘Does Entrepreneurial Self-Efficacy Distinguish Entrepreneurs from Managers?’, Journal of Business Venturing, 13(4), pp. 295–316.

Slibrary 6 — Entrepreneurial Mindset

Opportunity Recognition Model

A model explaining how entrepreneurs identify opportunities through the intersection of prior knowledge, alertness to information asymmetries, and social networks that expose them to diverse information flows.
How prior knowledge, alertness, and networks combine to reveal opportunities
Prior knowledge → domain expertise creates a lens for seeing opportunities others miss
Entrepreneurial alertness → sensitivity to information about unmet needs and resources
Social networks → weak ties expose entrepreneurs to novel, non-redundant information
Pattern recognition → connecting dots across industries, markets, and technologies
Reference

Shane, S. (2000) ‘Prior Knowledge and the Discovery of Entrepreneurial Opportunities’, Organization Science, 11(4), pp. 448–469.

Slibrary 7 — Strategic Responses to Disruption

RPV Framework

A diagnostic tool for assessing whether an organisation can successfully address a disruptive challenge by examining its Resources (what it has), Processes (how it works), and Values (what it prioritises).
Resources, Processes, Values — why capabilities become disabilities
Resources → tangible and intangible assets (people, tech, cash, relationships)
Processes → patterns of interaction, coordination, and decision-making
Values → criteria by which employees prioritise decisions (margins, market size)
Migration path → as firms mature, capability shifts from resources to processes to values
Reference

Christensen, C.M. and Raynor, M.E. (2003) The Innovator’s Solution. Boston, Mass.: Harvard Business School Press, pp. 177–206.

Slibrary 7 — Strategic Responses to Disruption

Crossing the Chasm

Moore’s model identifying the dangerous gap between early adopters (visionaries) and the early majority (pragmatists) — the point where most innovative products fail because they cannot transition from niche to mainstream.
The gap between visionaries and pragmatists where most innovations die
Early market → technology enthusiasts and visionaries buy the vision
The Chasm → pragmatists need references, whole products, and proven ROI
Bowling alley → target one niche segment, dominate it, then expand
Whole product → pragmatists buy complete solutions, not raw technology
Reference

Moore, G.A. (2014) Crossing the Chasm: Marketing and Selling Disruptive Products to Mainstream Customers. 3rd edn. New York: HarperBusiness.

Slibrary 7 — Strategic Responses to Disruption

Whole Product Model

Moore’s framework distinguishing the generic product (core technology) from the whole product (everything needed for the customer to achieve their buying objective) — essential for crossing the chasm.
Generic product vs. everything customers need for a complete solution
Generic product → what ships in the box, the core technology
Expected product → minimum requirements to meet purchase objective
Augmented product → maximum chance of achieving the buying objective
Potential product → room for growth as the market evolves
Reference

Moore, G.A. (2014) Crossing the Chasm. 3rd edn. New York: HarperBusiness, pp. 108–132.

Slibrary 7 — Strategic Responses to Disruption

Incumbent Response Framework

A classification of how established firms typically respond to disruptive threats: ignore, fight with sustaining innovation, create an autonomous unit, or acquire the disruptor — with varying degrees of success.
Four typical incumbent responses to disruption — and why most fail
Ignore → dismiss the threat as low-quality or niche (most common initial response)
Fight → improve existing products to outperform the disruptor (sustaining response)
Separate → create an autonomous business unit with different economics
Acquire → buy the disruptor before it becomes a competitive threat
Reference

Charitou, C.D. and Markides, C.C. (2003) ‘Responses to Disruptive Strategic Innovation’, MIT Sloan Management Review, 44(2), pp. 55–63.

Slibrary 7 — Strategic Responses to Disruption

Bowling Alley Strategy

Moore’s post-chasm growth strategy: dominate one niche segment completely (the head pin), then use that reference base to knock down adjacent segments sequentially rather than pursuing the mass market all at once.
Dominate one niche, then use it as a reference to capture adjacent segments
Head pin → the first niche segment with a compelling, urgent problem
Whole product → build the complete solution for that one segment
Reference base → satisfied customers in one niche create credibility for the next
Sequential domination → adjacent segments fall like bowling pins
Reference

Moore, G.A. (2014) Crossing the Chasm. 3rd edn. New York: HarperBusiness, pp. 67–95.

Slibrary 8 — Disruption in Context

Social Dimensions of Disruption

A framework for analysing how social factors — changing demographics, shifting values, inequality, and social movements — create conditions for disruptive innovation or shape how disruption is adopted and resisted.
How demographics, values, inequality, and movements shape disruption
Demographic shifts → ageing populations, urbanisation, migration patterns
Value shifts → sustainability, privacy, authenticity drive new market demands
Inequality dynamics → disruption can widen or narrow socioeconomic gaps
Social movements → collective action accelerates or blocks adoption
Reference

Soltanifar, M., Hughes, M. and Göcke, L. (2021) Digital Entrepreneurship: Impact on Business and Society. Cham: Springer, pp. 1–23.

Slibrary 8 — Disruption in Context

Political & Regulatory Disruption

A model examining how government policy, regulation, and political shifts create both barriers and opportunities for disruptive innovation — from the EU AI Act to antitrust enforcement to industrial policy.
How policy, regulation, and politics create barriers and opportunities for disruption
Regulatory barriers → compliance costs that protect incumbents from entrants
Regulatory enablers → new rules that mandate innovation (e.g. emissions standards)
Political risk → policy uncertainty that deters long-term investment
Industrial policy → government-backed disruption (e.g. CHIPS Act, EU Green Deal)
Reference

Blind, K. (2012) ‘The Influence of Regulations on Innovation’, Research Policy, 41(2), pp. 391–407.

Slibrary 8 — Disruption in Context

Cultural Innovation Model

Holt’s framework arguing that the most successful disruptive brands do not just offer better products — they offer better ideologies, addressing cultural contradictions that incumbents ignore or reinforce.
Disruptive brands offer better ideologies, not just better products
Cultural orthodoxy → the dominant ideology that incumbents embody and defend
Cultural contradiction → tensions in society that the orthodoxy cannot resolve
Cultural innovation → a new ideology that resolves the contradiction
Source material → subcultures, media myths, and social movements as raw material
Reference

Holt, D.B. (2004) How Brands Become Icons: The Principles of Cultural Branding. Boston, Mass.: Harvard Business School Press.

Slibrary 8 — Disruption in Context

Sustainability-Driven Disruption

A framework showing how environmental constraints, circular economy principles, and ESG mandates are creating a new class of disruption — where sustainability is not a cost centre but a competitive advantage.
Environmental constraints and ESG mandates as drivers of disruptive innovation
Circular economy → disrupting linear take-make-waste models
ESG mandates → regulation forcing incumbents to transform or face penalties
Green premium → the cost gap between sustainable and conventional that disruptors close
Impact investing → capital flows favouring sustainable disruptors over incumbents
Reference

Schaltegger, S., Lüdeke-Freund, F. and Hansen, E.G. (2012) ‘Business Cases for Sustainability’, International Journal of Innovation and Sustainable Development, 6(2), pp. 95–119.

Slibrary 8 — Disruption in Context

Digital Platform Disruption

A model showing how digital platforms disrupt traditional industries by shifting competition from product-vs-product to ecosystem-vs-ecosystem, leveraging data network effects, and collapsing traditional value chains.
Ecosystem-vs-ecosystem competition that collapses traditional value chains
Pipeline to platform → shift from linear value chains to multi-sided networks
Ecosystem competition → platforms compete as ecosystems, not individual firms
Data advantage → platform data creates compounding competitive moats
Disintermediation → platforms remove intermediaries, capturing their margin
Reference

Parker, G., Van Alstyne, M. and Choudary, S.P. (2016) Platform Revolution. New York: W.W. Norton, pp. 1–28.
