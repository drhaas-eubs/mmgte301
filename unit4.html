Slibrary 13 — Business Planning

Business Plan Architecture

A comprehensive framework for structuring a traditional business plan: executive summary, company description, market analysis, organisation, product/service line, marketing strategy, and financial projections.
Seven-section structure from executive summary to financial projections
Executive summary → one-page overview that sells the entire plan
Market analysis → industry size, trends, target segment, competitive landscape
Organisation → team, legal structure, advisory board, key hires
Marketing strategy → positioning, pricing, distribution, customer acquisition
Financial projections → revenue model, P&L forecast, cash flow, break-even analysis
Reference

Hisrich, R.D., Peters, M.P. and Shepherd, D.A. (2020) Entrepreneurship. 11th edn. New York: McGraw-Hill, pp. 198–232.

Slibrary 13 — Business Planning

Lean Business Plan

A streamlined planning approach that replaces the traditional 40-page business plan with a living, one-page document focused on hypotheses, experiments, and validated learning.
Iterative one-page plan for early-stage ventures replacing traditional planning
Strategy → market, value proposition, competitive advantage in bullet points
Tactics → pricing, channels, key activities for the next 90 days
Business model → revenue streams, cost structure, key metrics
Schedule → milestones, responsibilities, review dates
Reference

Maurya, A. (2012) Running Lean. 2nd edn. Sebastopol, CA: O’Reilly Media.

Slibrary 13 — Business Planning

Financial Projections Model

A financial planning framework linking three core statements — income statement (profitability), balance sheet (financial position), and cash flow statement (liquidity) — with assumptions clearly stated.
Three-statement model: income statement, balance sheet, cash flow
Income statement → revenue, COGS, gross margin, operating expenses, net profit
Balance sheet → assets, liabilities, equity at a point in time
Cash flow → operating, investing, and financing cash flows
Assumptions → the explicit hypotheses underlying every projection
Reference

Berman, K. and Knight, J. (2013) Financial Intelligence for Entrepreneurs. Boston: Harvard Business Review Press.

Slibrary 13 — Business Planning

Kawasaki 10-Slide Pitch Deck

Guy Kawasaki’s framework for the ideal venture pitch: ten slides, twenty minutes, thirty-point font minimum — forcing founders to distil their venture to its essence.
Ten essential slides for a compelling investor presentation
Title → company name, tagline, contact
Problem/Solution → what pain exists and how you solve it
Business model → how you make money
Technology/Magic → your secret sauce or unfair advantage
Marketing → how you reach customers; Team → why this team wins
Financials → revenue, expenses, milestones; Ask → what you need
Reference

Kawasaki, G. (2015) The Art of the Start 2.0. New York: Portfolio/Penguin.

Slibrary 13 — Business Planning

Executive Summary Framework

A structured template for the single most important page of any business plan — the executive summary that determines whether investors read further or stop.
One-page synthesis that sells the entire venture in five minutes
Hook → one sentence that captures the opportunity
Problem → the specific pain point and who experiences it
Solution → what you offer and why it is better
Market → size, growth, and your target segment
Traction → evidence of progress (users, revenue, partnerships)
Ask → funding amount, use of proceeds, expected milestones
Reference

Hisrich, R.D., Peters, M.P. and Shepherd, D.A. (2020) Entrepreneurship. 11th edn. New York: McGraw-Hill, pp. 200–205.

Slibrary 14 — Funding & Finance

Startup Funding Stages

A roadmap of venture funding stages, each with distinct investor types, capital amounts, valuation expectations, and milestones required for progression.
Progressive capital rounds from pre-seed through Series A/B/C to IPO
Pre-seed → founders’ savings, friends and family (€10K–€250K)
Seed → angel investors, accelerators (€250K–€2M) for MVP and early traction
Series A → venture capital (€2M–€15M) for product-market fit and scaling
Series B/C → growth capital (€15M–€100M+) for market expansion
IPO/Exit → public listing or acquisition as liquidity event
Reference

Feld, B. and Mendelson, J. (2019) Venture Deals: Be Smarter Than Your Lawyer and Venture Capitalist. 4th edn. Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons.

Slibrary 14 — Funding & Finance

Bootstrapping vs. VC Decision Tree

A decision tree helping founders evaluate whether to bootstrap (self-fund) or seek venture capital based on market timing, capital intensity, growth expectations, and founder preferences.
Strategic decision framework for choosing between self-funding and external capital
Capital intensity → can the business reach profitability on founder resources?
Market timing → is there a first-mover window that requires speed?
Growth expectations → does the opportunity support venture-scale returns (10x+)?
Control preference → how much equity dilution and board oversight is acceptable?
Reference

Wasserman, N. (2012) The Founder’s Dilemmas: Anticipating and Avoiding the Pitfalls That Can Sink a Startup. Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press.

Slibrary 14 — Funding & Finance

Unit Economics Dashboard

A financial health dashboard tracking the fundamental economics of each customer: how much it costs to acquire them (CAC), how much revenue they generate (LTV), and how long until the investment is recovered.
Key per-unit metrics: CAC, LTV, ARPU, churn, payback period
Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) → total sales and marketing spend per new customer
Lifetime Value (LTV) → total revenue expected from a customer over their lifetime
LTV:CAC ratio → must be >3:1 for a sustainable business
Payback period → months to recover CAC from customer revenue
Churn rate → percentage of customers lost per period
Reference

Skok, D. (2019) ‘SaaS Metrics 2.0’, For Entrepreneurs. Available at: https://www.forentrepreneurs.com.

Slibrary 14 — Funding & Finance

Burn Rate & Runway Calculator

A financial planning tool calculating how fast a startup spends its cash (burn rate) and how many months of operation remain at the current spending rate (runway).
Monthly cash consumption and months until capital runs out
Gross burn → total monthly cash expenditure
Net burn → monthly expenditure minus monthly revenue
Runway → cash balance divided by net monthly burn
Default alive → will current growth trajectory reach profitability before cash runs out?
Reference

Graham, P. (2015) ‘Default Alive or Default Dead?’, Paul Graham’s Essays. Available at: http://www.paulgraham.com.

Slibrary 14 — Funding & Finance

Cap Table Structure

A capitalisation table documenting who owns what percentage of a company, how ownership has changed through funding rounds, and what rights and preferences each class of shares carries.
Equity ownership table tracking shares, dilution, and investor rights
Founder equity → initial ownership split, vesting schedules, cliff periods
Option pool → equity reserved for employee stock options (typically 10–20%)
Investor equity → preferred shares with liquidation preferences and anti-dilution
Dilution mechanics → how each funding round reduces existing shareholders’ percentages
Reference

Feld, B. and Mendelson, J. (2019) Venture Deals. 4th edn. Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons, pp. 39–72.

Slibrary 15 — Growth & Scaling

Startup Growth Lifecycle

A lifecycle model describing the four phases every startup passes through, each requiring fundamentally different strategies, metrics, team structures, and leadership styles.
Four stages: search, build, grow, scale — each with different priorities
Search → finding product-market fit (pre-revenue, hypothesis-driven)
Build → constructing the repeatable sales model (early revenue, unit economics)
Grow → accelerating customer acquisition (scaling channels, hiring)
Scale → optimising operations for efficiency (processes, culture, international)
Reference

Marmer, M. et al. (2011) Startup Genome Report. Startup Compass Inc.

Slibrary 15 — Growth & Scaling

Growth Flywheel

A strategic model showing how growth compounds through a self-reinforcing cycle: more customers generate more data/revenue, enabling better products, which attract more customers.
Self-reinforcing growth loop where each element accelerates the next
Value creation → the product or service improvement that attracts users
Customer acquisition → channels that bring new users to the product
Monetisation → converting user engagement into revenue
Reinvestment → using revenue to improve the product and fuel the cycle
Reference

Collins, J. (2001) Good to Great: Why Some Companies Make the Leap and Others Don’t. New York: HarperBusiness, pp. 164–187.

Slibrary 15 — Growth & Scaling

Franchise Model

A growth and scaling framework where the franchisor licenses a proven business model, brand, and operating system to independent franchisees who invest their own capital and manage local operations.
Replicating a proven business system through licensed independent operators
Franchise agreement → legal contract defining rights, obligations, and fees
Operations manual → standardised procedures ensuring consistency across locations
Fee structure → initial franchise fee plus ongoing royalties (typically 4–8% of revenue)
Quality control → systems for maintaining brand standards across independent operators
Reference

Hisrich, R.D., Peters, M.P. and Shepherd, D.A. (2020) Entrepreneurship. 11th edn. New York: McGraw-Hill, pp. 415–440.

Slibrary 15 — Growth & Scaling

Scaling Readiness Matrix

A checklist framework evaluating a startup’s readiness to scale across five critical dimensions: product-market fit evidence, unit economics, team capability, operational infrastructure, and capital access.
Diagnostic tool assessing whether a venture is ready to scale across five dimensions
Product-market fit → retention metrics confirm customers value the product
Unit economics → LTV:CAC > 3:1 with acceptable payback period
Team → key roles filled, culture defined, hiring pipeline established
Operations → systems, processes, and technology that can handle 10x volume
Capital → funding secured or revenue sufficient to finance growth
Reference

Blank, S. (2013) ‘Why the Lean Start-Up Changes Everything’, Harvard Business Review, 91(5), pp. 63–72.

Slibrary 15 — Growth & Scaling

Customer Acquisition Funnel

A marketing and sales framework tracking how potential customers move through stages of engagement — from awareness through consideration, conversion, and retention — with conversion rates measured at each stage.
Stage-by-stage conversion model from awareness to retention
Awareness → potential customers learn you exist (impressions, reach)
Consideration → potential customers evaluate your offering (site visits, trials)
Conversion → prospects become paying customers (sign-ups, purchases)
Retention → customers continue using and paying (repeat purchase, renewal)
Referral → satisfied customers bring new customers (viral coefficient)
Reference

McClure, D. (2007) ‘Startup Metrics for Pirates: AARRR!’, 500 Startups. Presented at Ignite Seattle.

Slibrary 16 — Exit & Value Creation

Exit Strategy Options

A framework mapping the available exit pathways for founders and investors, each with different implications for valuation, control, timeline, and stakeholder outcomes.
Five pathways: IPO, acquisition, merger, MBO, liquidation
IPO → public listing providing liquidity and brand visibility (highest valuation potential)
Strategic acquisition → sale to a larger company seeking capabilities or market access
Financial acquisition → sale to private equity seeking operational returns
Management buyout (MBO) → existing management team purchases the company
Liquidation → orderly wind-down and distribution of remaining assets
Reference

DeTienne, D.R. (2010) ‘Entrepreneurial Exit as a Critical Component of the Entrepreneurial Process’, Journal of Business Venturing, 25(2), pp. 203–215.

Slibrary 16 — Exit & Value Creation

Valuation Methods

A toolkit of valuation approaches used at different venture stages — discounted cash flow for established businesses, comparable multiples for benchmarking, and the VC method for early-stage companies.
Three approaches: DCF, comparable multiples, and VC method
Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) → present value of projected future cash flows
Comparable multiples → valuation based on similar companies (revenue, EBITDA multiples)
VC method → target return divided into projected exit value to determine pre-money valuation
Stage-adjusted risk → higher discount rates for earlier-stage ventures
Reference

Damodaran, A. (2012) Investment Valuation: Tools and Techniques for Determining the Value of Any Asset. 3rd edn. Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons.

Slibrary 16 — Exit & Value Creation

Buy-Out Structures

A framework comparing buy-out structures for venture transitions — management buyouts, leveraged buyouts, and employee ownership models — each with different financing, governance, and outcome implications.
Three models: MBO, LBO, and employee ownership transitions
Management Buyout (MBO) → management team acquires the business using debt and equity
Leveraged Buyout (LBO) → acquisition funded primarily with debt secured against company assets
Employee Ownership (ESOP/EOT) → employees acquire ownership through a trust structure
Financing mix → the ratio of debt to equity determines risk and return profiles
Reference

Wright, M. and Robbie, K. (1998) ‘Venture Capital and Private Equity: A Review and Synthesis’, Journal of Business Finance & Accounting, 25(5–6), pp. 521–570.

Slibrary 16 — Exit & Value Creation

Strategic Acquisition Framework

A structured approach to acquiring companies strategically, covering target identification, valuation, due diligence, deal negotiation, and post-merger integration.
Four-stage process: target identification, due diligence, negotiation, integration
Target identification → screening for strategic fit, capability gaps, or market access
Due diligence → financial, legal, operational, and cultural assessment
Deal structure → purchase price, earn-outs, retention agreements, representations
Post-merger integration → combining operations, cultures, and systems (where most deals fail)
Reference

Haspeslagh, P.C. and Jemison, D.B. (1991) Managing Acquisitions: Creating Value Through Corporate Renewal. New York: Free Press.

Slibrary 16 — Exit & Value Creation

Intrapreneurship Model

A framework for fostering entrepreneurial activity within large organisations — using corporate resources, capabilities, and market access to create new ventures while managing the tension between innovation and operational efficiency.
Creating new ventures within established organisations using corporate resources
Autonomous teams → separate units with distinct cultures, metrics, and decision-making authority
Corporate sponsorship → senior leadership protection and resource commitment
Venture boards → internal governance structures mimicking investor oversight
Spin-off readiness → criteria for deciding when internal ventures should become independent
Reference

Burgelman, R.A. (1983) ‘Corporate Entrepreneurship and Strategic Management’, Management Science, 29(12), pp. 1349–1364.
