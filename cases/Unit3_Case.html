Case Study
Airbnb: From Air Mattresses to a $75 Billion Platform
How an idea crossed the chasm

In October 2007, Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia could not afford rent on their San Francisco apartment. A major design conference was coming to the city, and hotels were fully booked. They bought three air mattresses, set up a simple website called AirBed & Breakfast, and offered attendees a place to sleep for $80 per night including breakfast. Three guests booked.

The pain point was real: hotels in major cities were expensive, impersonal, and frequently unavailable during peak events. The innovation was radical in its simplicity: turn spare rooms, apartments, and houses into accommodation. The hosts were already there. The supply was already built. The platform simply needed to connect supply with demand.

Airbnb’s earliest adopters were exactly who Rogers’ diffusion theory would predict: young, price-sensitive travellers (innovators and early adopters) who valued experience over predictability. The company’s first critical growth hack came from cross-posting listings to Craigslist, piggybacking on an existing network to bootstrap its own.

Crossing the chasm — moving from adventurous early adopters to the pragmatic early majority — required solving the trust problem. Airbnb introduced verified photos, host/guest reviews, a messaging system, and eventually a $1 million host guarantee. Each feature was a brick in Moore’s “whole product” — the complete solution that pragmatists need before they will adopt.

By 2014, Airbnb had 800,000 listings in 190 countries. By its 2020 IPO, the company was valued at $47 billion. In 2024, its market capitalisation exceeded $75 billion, larger than Marriott and Hilton combined. The three air mattresses had become a global platform.
