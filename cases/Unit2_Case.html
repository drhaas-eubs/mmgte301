Case Study
Blockbuster vs. Netflix: The Five Laws in Action
How rational management led to irrational failure

In 2004, Blockbuster Video was the undisputed king of home entertainment. With over 9,000 stores worldwide, 84,000 employees, and annual revenues exceeding $6 billion, the company commanded an 85% market share in video rental. Its brand was a cultural icon.

Netflix, founded in 1997 by Reed Hastings and Marc Randolph, had a radically different model: DVDs by mail, no late fees, and a monthly subscription. By 2004, Netflix had grown to 4 million subscribers but generated only $500 million in revenue — less than one-tenth of Blockbuster’s. To Blockbuster’s management, Netflix was a niche player serving an underserved segment of customers who preferred convenience over selection.

Blockbuster’s response was textbook sustaining innovation. In 2004, it launched Blockbuster Online, a direct copy of Netflix’s DVD-by-mail service, and briefly surpassed Netflix in online subscriber growth. But the initiative was starved of resources: every dollar spent on online cannibalised the company’s enormously profitable store network. Late fees alone generated approximately $800 million annually — 16% of total revenue. Blockbuster’s franchisees, landlords, and Wall Street analysts all demanded that the company protect its physical retail model.

Internally, Blockbuster possessed the resources to compete: cash, brand, customer data, and content relationships. What it lacked were the processes (optimised for physical retail, not digital logistics) and the values (high-margin expectations that made low-margin subscription economics unacceptable). The company’s RPV framework — its capabilities — simultaneously defined its disabilities.

By 2010, Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy. Netflix had 20 million subscribers and was transitioning to streaming. The company that could have bought Netflix for $50 million in 2000 was destroyed by a business model it understood perfectly but could not adopt.
