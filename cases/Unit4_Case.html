Case Study
Revolut: Building a Fintech Unicorn at Speed
Venture process from MVP to £33 billion valuation

In July 2015, Nikolay Storonsky, a former Credit Suisse trader, and Vlad Yatsenko, a former Deutsche Bank developer, launched Revolut with a single proposition: a prepaid card that let users exchange currencies at the interbank rate with no fees. The MVP was deliberately narrow — one feature, executed well, solving a specific pain point for frequent travellers.

The founders bootstrapped the initial development with £300,000 of their own savings and a small friends-and-family round. Within six months, Revolut had attracted 100,000 users through word-of-mouth and a viral referral programme. The unit economics were brutal at this stage — customer acquisition cost (CAC) was near zero (organic growth), but average revenue per user (ARPU) was minimal because the core product was free.

Revolut’s Series A (£4.8 million, 2016) funded the expansion from currency exchange into a broader fintech platform: savings vaults, crypto trading, business accounts, and insurance. Each new feature was tested as a minimum viable product, measured against user engagement metrics, and either scaled or killed. By 2018, Revolut had raised $250 million in Series C at a $1.7 billion valuation — unicorn status achieved in just three years.

The company’s growth strategy followed a clear pattern: acquire users with a free core product, monetise through premium subscriptions (£7.99/month for Plus, £13.99 for Metal), and cross-sell financial products. By 2024, Revolut had over 35 million customers, had obtained a UK banking licence, and achieved a valuation of £33 billion in its secondary share sale.

Revolut’s story illustrates the full venture process: from a validated pain point to an MVP, through multiple funding rounds, to a scaled platform with diversified revenue streams and a path toward public listing.
