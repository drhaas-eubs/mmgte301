Case Study
Kodak: The Giant That Saw It Coming
Incumbent with the patent that couldn’t save it

In 1975, Steven Sasson, a young engineer at Eastman Kodak, built the world’s first digital camera. It weighed 3.6 kilograms, captured a 0.01-megapixel black-and-white image, and took 23 seconds to record to a cassette tape. When Sasson presented his invention to Kodak’s management, their response was unequivocal: “That’s cute — but don’t tell anyone about it.”

By 2003, Kodak was still the world’s fourth most valuable brand, generating $13.3 billion in revenue. Its film and printing business employed over 60,000 people. The company held over 1,000 digital imaging patents — more than almost any competitor. It was not ignorant of the digital transition; it was structurally incapable of pursuing it.

Kodak’s film division operated on gross margins of approximately 60–70%. Digital cameras, by contrast, were a commodity hardware business with margins below 10%. Every dollar invested in digital cannibalised the company’s most profitable product line. Kodak’s largest customers — photo labs, retailers, and professional photographers — depended on the film ecosystem and actively resisted the shift. Wall Street rewarded Kodak for its film margins and punished any signal of strategic uncertainty.

The company’s response was a classic sustaining innovation strategy: it invested in hybrid products like the Advantix Preview camera (film with a digital preview screen), launched the Kodak EasyShare line of digital cameras, and acquired the online photo service Ofoto. Each move was logical within Kodak’s existing value network. None addressed the fundamental disruption: that digital photography would eventually make film-based photography obsolete, destroying the razor-and-blade model (cheap cameras, expensive film and processing) that had sustained Kodak for over a century.

By 2005, digital camera sales surpassed film cameras globally. By 2010, Kodak’s revenue had fallen to $7.2 billion. In January 2012, Eastman Kodak filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection. The company that invented digital photography was destroyed by digital photography.

Kodak’s story is not one of ignorance or incompetence. Its engineers understood the technology. Its strategists saw the market shift. Its leaders made decisions that were rational within their existing value network. The failure was structural: the organisation was optimised for a business model that the innovation it had created would eventually destroy.
