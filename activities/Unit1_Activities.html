1

The Disruption Drop

10 min

Your facilitator will present a real-world disruption scenario — a provocation, not a lecture.

Your task: Listen. Then sit in silence for 2 minutes. Think about the problem. Do not write. Do not discuss. Just think.

After the silence, 2–3 of you will be asked: “What came to mind?” There is no right answer yet — the discomfort of not having a framework ready is the point.

2

Gallery Exploration

20 min

Open: Unit 1 Framework Gallery on your device (laptop or phone).

The gallery contains 20 frameworks across 4 Slibraries (1–4). Work in pairs.

Your task: Find the 3 frameworks that best help you respond to the challenge from Phase 1. Click into each one — read the definition and the source. Write down:
• Framework name
• Slibrary number
• One sentence: why did you choose this one?

You have 15 minutes. Try to select frameworks from different Slibraries — combining lenses is more powerful than choosing three from the same set.

3

Framework Pitch

15 min

3–4 pairs will present their selections to the group.

Your task: Name your 3 frameworks, state which Slibrary each comes from, and explain in one sentence why you chose that combination. You have 90 seconds.

The facilitator will challenge your choices — not to find fault, but to push your thinking. Expect questions like: “You chose market analysis frameworks but no strategy framework — how will you act on the analysis?”

4

Case Deep Dive: Kodak

40 min

Kodak: The Giant That Saw It Coming

You are an external strategy consultant presenting to the Kodak board in 2003. The company invented the digital camera in 1975 but chose not to develop it. Using frameworks from the gallery, prepare a visual argument recommending a strategic direction for Kodak’s $14 billion business.

Your task: Form teams of 4. Using at least two frameworks from the gallery, produce a one-page visual argument — sketches, diagrams, and arrows, not paragraphs. You have 35 minutes (5 to read, 30 to build).

Your visual argument should contain: a diagnosis (what is the current state?), an analysis (why is this happening?), and a recommendation (what should be done?).

Tip: Annotate your framework sketches with specific data from the case. The best arguments connect frameworks to each other with arrows showing cause and effect.
5

Board Presentations

20 min
Your task: Present your visual argument at the whiteboard. You have 90 seconds. Strict time limit. Sketch, point, explain — then stop.

You will be evaluated on: framework selection (did you use multiple Slibraries?), visual clarity (can we follow your logic?), case specificity (did you use data from the case?), and recommendation quality (is it clear and actionable?).

After all presentations, the facilitator will synthesise the common patterns and key differences across teams.

☕ Break — 10 minutes
6

Second Scenario

25 min

A contrasting scenario to test whether different problems pull different frameworks forward.

A Berlin-based startup called LernAI has raised €1.5 million in pre-seed funding to build an AI-powered language learning app. They have 2,000 beta users but face fierce competition from Duolingo (450 million users) and Babbel. Their technology is superior in pronunciation correction, but they have no brand, no distribution, and limited runway. How do they survive the next 12 months?

Your task: Return to the gallery. Focus on Slibraries 3 and 4 (Market Dynamics, Innovation Strategy). In your pair, identify the 2–3 frameworks most relevant to this new scenario. Be ready to explain why different frameworks are needed here than in the Kodak case.

The debrief will highlight how the same gallery serves completely different problems — the skill is matching the Slibrary to the bottleneck.

7

Reflection & Personal Slibrary

10 min

Individually. No discussion. Answer three questions in writing:

Question 1: Which framework surprised you most today?

Question 2: Which two frameworks would you reach for first in a real meeting?

Question 3: Which framework do you not yet fully understand? (This becomes your independent study task — return to the gallery, read the source, and write a 200-word explanation for next session.)

Complete the full reflection prompt (available on the course page) before the next session. Bring your written responses — they open our next discussion.
