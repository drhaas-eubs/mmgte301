Slibrary 1 — Innovation Foundations

Christensen’s Disruption S-Curves

A model contrasting two performance trajectories — the incumbent’s sustaining path and the entrant’s disruptive path — showing how inferior products cross the market-need threshold and displace established competitors.
Incumbent vs. entrant performance trajectories over time
Sustaining trajectory → continuous improvement along dimensions existing customers value
Disruptive trajectory → starts below market needs, improves rapidly
Market need threshold → the performance level mainstream customers require
Overshoot zone → where incumbents exceed what customers will pay for
Reference

Christensen, C.M. (2016) The Innovator’s Dilemma: When New Technologies Cause Great Firms to Fail. Boston, Mass.: Harvard Business Review Press.

Slibrary 1 — Innovation Foundations

Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation

A two-path classification distinguishing innovations that improve existing products for current customers (sustaining) from those that introduce a different value proposition, initially serving overlooked segments before capturing the mainstream (disruptive).
Two-path comparison: performance improvement vs. market creation
Sustaining path → targets existing customers with incremental or breakthrough improvements
Low-end disruption → targets overserved customers with simpler, cheaper alternatives
New-market disruption → targets non-consumers, creating entirely new demand
Reference

Christensen, C.M., Raynor, M.E. and McDonald, R. (2015) ‘What Is Disruptive Innovation?’, Harvard Business Review, 93(12), pp. 44–53.

Slibrary 1 — Innovation Foundations

Technology S-Curve

A model plotting technological performance against cumulative effort, revealing three phases: slow emergence, rapid growth, and diminishing-return maturity — signalling when a successor technology is likely to appear.
Performance vs. effort: emergence, growth, and maturity phases
Emergence → slow initial progress, high uncertainty
Growth → rapid performance gains, market excitement
Maturity → diminishing returns, incremental improvements only
Discontinuity → the jump from one S-curve to the next
Reference

Foster, R.N. (1986) Innovation: The Attacker’s Advantage. New York: Summit Books.

Slibrary 1 — Innovation Foundations

Innovation Spectrum

A four-level classification of innovation from incremental improvements through architectural reconfiguration and radical breakthroughs to disruptive market creation, each demanding a different strategic response.
Incremental → Architectural → Radical → Disruptive
Incremental → minor improvements to existing products (e.g. iPhone 15 to 16)
Architectural → reconfiguring components in new ways (e.g. Tesla battery platform)
Radical → fundamentally new technology (e.g. CRISPR gene editing)
Disruptive → creating new markets that displace incumbents (e.g. Airbnb)
Reference

Henderson, R.M. and Clark, K.B. (1990) ‘Architectural Innovation: The Reconfiguration of Existing Product Technologies and the Failure of Established Firms’, Administrative Science Quarterly, 35(1), pp. 9–30.

Slibrary 1 — Innovation Foundations

Three Horizons of Growth

A portfolio framework proposing that companies must manage innovation across three concurrent time horizons: defending the core (H1), building emerging businesses (H2), and creating options for the future (H3).
McKinsey’s H1/H2/H3 portfolio across time horizons
Horizon 1 → defend and extend current business (existing P&L, current customers)
Horizon 2 → build emerging businesses (scaling pilots, adjacent markets)
Horizon 3 → create viable options (research, venture bets, disruptive experiments)
Reference

Baghai, M., Coley, S. and White, D. (1999) The Alchemy of Growth. London: Orion Business.

Slibrary 2 — Product & Platform Life Cycles

Product Life Cycle

A model describing the four stages a product passes through from launch to withdrawal — Introduction, Growth, Maturity, and Decline — each demanding a different strategic emphasis.
Introduction → Growth → Maturity → Decline
Introduction → high cost, low sales, market education required
Growth → rapid adoption, competitors enter, scale economics emerge
Maturity → market saturated, price competition, marginal differentiation
Decline → falling demand, exit decisions, potential for disruption
Reference

Levitt, T. (1965) ‘Exploit the Product Life Cycle’, Harvard Business Review, 43(6), pp. 81–94.

Slibrary 2 — Product & Platform Life Cycles

Technology Adoption Life Cycle

Rogers’ bell-curve model segmenting technology adopters into five categories — Innovators (2.5%), Early Adopters (13.5%), Early Majority (34%), Late Majority (34%), and Laggards (16%) — each with distinct buying motivations.
Rogers’ bell curve: Innovators to Laggards
Innovators (2.5%) → technology enthusiasts, risk-tolerant
Early Adopters (13.5%) → visionaries who see strategic value
Early Majority (34%) → pragmatists who need proven solutions
Late Majority (34%) → conservatives driven by peer pressure
Laggards (16%) → sceptics who adopt only when unavoidable
Reference

Rogers, E.M. (2003) Diffusion of Innovations. 5th edn. New York: Free Press.

Slibrary 2 — Product & Platform Life Cycles

Platform Business Model

A business model that creates value by facilitating exchange between two or more interdependent groups — typically producers and consumers — orchestrating interactions rather than creating and selling products linearly.
Two-sided markets connecting producers and consumers
Producers → supply-side participants who create value (drivers, hosts, developers)
Consumers → demand-side participants who consume value
Platform infrastructure → technology, rules, and governance enabling exchange
Network effects → cross-side and same-side value amplification
Reference

Parker, G., Van Alstyne, M. and Choudary, S.P. (2016) Platform Revolution. New York: W.W. Norton.

Slibrary 2 — Product & Platform Life Cycles

Network Effects Flywheel

A self-reinforcing cycle in which more users generate more data, which improves the product, which lowers per-unit costs, which attracts more users — creating winner-take-most dynamics.
Value increases with each additional user — virtuous cycle
Direct network effects → same-side value (more WhatsApp users = more useful)
Indirect network effects → cross-side value (more iOS apps attract more iPhone buyers)
Data network effects → more usage produces better algorithms (Google Search)
Tipping point → the threshold where network effects become self-sustaining
Reference

Shapiro, C. and Varian, H.R. (1999) Information Rules. Boston: Harvard Business School Press.

Slibrary 2 — Product & Platform Life Cycles

Gartner Hype Cycle

A five-phase model illustrating how a technology evolves through public and market perception: from innovation trigger through inflated expectations, disillusionment, enlightenment, and finally a plateau of measurable productivity.
Trigger → Peak → Trough → Slope → Plateau of Productivity
Innovation Trigger → breakthrough, proof of concept, media attention
Peak of Inflated Expectations → hype exceeds capability
Trough of Disillusionment → failures dominate the narrative
Slope of Enlightenment → practical use cases crystallise
Plateau of Productivity → mainstream adoption with clear ROI
Reference

Fenn, J. and Raskino, M. (2008) Mastering the Hype Cycle. Boston: Harvard Business Press.

Slibrary 3 — Market Dynamics

Creative Destruction

Schumpeter’s concept describing the process by which new innovations continuously destroy and replace older economic structures — each major technological wave creating new industries while rendering existing ones obsolete.
Schumpeter’s innovation waves — new industries destroy old ones
Innovation waves → steam, electricity, computing, internet, AI
Entrepreneurial function → the revolutionary who makes the old way impossible
Creative response → new combinations of resources that displace existing ones
Industrial mutation → the process that incessantly revolutionises economic structure
Reference

Schumpeter, J.A. (1942) Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy. New York: Harper & Brothers.

Slibrary 3 — Market Dynamics

Jobs-to-be-Done

A framework arguing that customers do not buy products — they hire them to get a functional, emotional, or social job done. Understanding the job, rather than customer demographics, reveals the true basis for competition.
Customers hire products to get a job done — functional, emotional, social
Functional job → the practical task the customer needs to accomplish
Emotional job → how the customer wants to feel
Social job → how the customer wants to be perceived by others
Overserved jobs → where current solutions exceed needs (disruption opportunity)
Reference

Christensen, C.M., Hall, T., Dillon, K. and Duncan, D.S. (2016) ‘Know Your Customers’ “Jobs to Be Done”’, Harvard Business Review, 94(9), pp. 54–62.

Slibrary 3 — Market Dynamics

Value Innovation — Blue Ocean Strategy

A strategy framework arguing that the most profitable growth comes from creating uncontested blue ocean market space through the Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create grid, rather than competing in crowded red ocean markets.
Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create grid for uncontested market space
Eliminate → remove factors the industry takes for granted
Reduce → lower factors well below the industry standard
Raise → increase factors well above the industry standard
Create → introduce factors the industry has never offered
Reference

Kim, W.C. and Mauborgne, R. (2015) Blue Ocean Strategy. Expanded edn. Boston: Harvard Business Review Press.

Slibrary 3 — Market Dynamics

First-Mover vs. Fast-Follower

A strategic analysis comparing the advantages of market pioneers (brand recognition, customer lock-in) against fast followers who learn from first-mover mistakes, enter with superior resources, and free-ride on market education.
When pioneering pays off vs. when learning from others wins
First-mover advantages → brand recognition, switching costs, standard-setting power
First-mover disadvantages → high R&D cost, market uncertainty, free-rider problem
Fast-follower advantages → learn from mistakes, better execution, lower risk
Moderating factors → switching costs, network effects, learning curve steepness
Reference

Lieberman, M.B. and Montgomery, D.B. (1988) ‘First-Mover Advantages’, Strategic Management Journal, 9(S1), pp. 41–58.

Slibrary 3 — Market Dynamics

Porter’s Five Forces

An industry analysis model examining five structural forces — threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitutes, and competitive rivalry — that together determine profit potential.
Industry structure: rivalry, buyers, suppliers, entrants, substitutes
Threat of new entrants → barriers to entry determine competitive pressure
Bargaining power of suppliers → concentrated suppliers can squeeze margins
Bargaining power of buyers → powerful buyers force down prices
Threat of substitutes → alternative products serving the same function
Competitive rivalry → intensity of competition among existing firms
Reference

Porter, M.E. (2008) ‘The Five Competitive Forces That Shape Strategy’, Harvard Business Review, 86(1), pp. 78–93.

Slibrary 4 — Innovation Strategy

Lean Startup Loop

A methodology replacing elaborate business planning with a rapid cycle of validated learning: build a minimum viable product, measure how customers respond using actionable metrics, and learn whether to pivot or persevere.
Build → Measure → Learn — validated learning through rapid iteration
Build → create a minimum viable product to test a hypothesis
Measure → collect actionable metrics on customer behaviour
Learn → decide whether to pivot (change direction) or persevere
Innovation accounting → metrics that track validated learning, not vanity metrics
Reference

Ries, E. (2011) The Lean Startup. New York: Crown Business.

Slibrary 4 — Innovation Strategy

Business Model Canvas

A strategic tool mapping nine interconnected building blocks — Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partnerships, and Cost Structure — on a single page.
Osterwalder’s 9-block strategic blueprint
Value Proposition → the central block connecting what you offer to who you serve
Customer Segments → the distinct groups of people or organisations you serve
Revenue Streams → how the business captures value from each segment
Cost Structure → the most important costs inherent in the business model
Reference

Osterwalder, A. and Pigneur, Y. (2010) Business Model Generation. Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons.

Slibrary 4 — Innovation Strategy

Value Proposition Canvas

A detailed lens on two blocks of the Business Model Canvas: the Customer Profile (jobs, pains, gains) and the Value Map (products, pain relievers, gain creators). Fit is achieved when the Value Map addresses the most important customer jobs.
Mapping product features to customer jobs, pains, and gains
Customer jobs → what the customer is trying to get done
Customer pains → negative outcomes, risks, and obstacles
Customer gains → desired outcomes, benefits, and aspirations
Fit → when pain relievers and gain creators match the most important jobs
Reference

Osterwalder, A., Pigneur, Y., Bernarda, G. and Smith, A. (2014) Value Proposition Design. Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons.

Slibrary 4 — Innovation Strategy

Ambidextrous Organisation

An organisational design model for simultaneously pursuing exploitation (refining existing products for efficiency) and exploration (searching for new opportunities through experimentation), with structural separation and tight senior leadership integration.
Simultaneously exploiting current business and exploring new opportunities
Exploit → efficiency, control, certainty, incremental improvement
Explore → search, autonomy, experimentation, radical innovation
Structural separation → distinct units with different cultures, metrics, and incentives
Senior leadership integration → shared vision and resource allocation at the top
Reference

O’Reilly, C.A. and Tushman, M.L. (2016) Lead and Disrupt. Stanford, CA: Stanford Business Books.

Slibrary 4 — Innovation Strategy

Innovation Portfolio Matrix

A resource allocation framework suggesting that firms achieving the highest returns invest roughly 70% in core innovations, 20% in adjacent innovations, and 10% in transformational innovations — with the returns ratio inverted.
70/20/10 allocation: Core · Adjacent · Transformational
Core (70%) → incremental improvements to existing offerings for existing customers
Adjacent (20%) → expanding into related markets or capabilities
Transformational (10%) → creating entirely new markets or disruptive offerings
Inverted returns → the 10% allocation often generates 70% of long-term value
Reference

Nagji, B. and Tuff, G. (2012) ‘Managing Your Innovation Portfolio’, Harvard Business Review, 90(5), pp. 66–74.
